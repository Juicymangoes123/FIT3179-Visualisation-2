Patterns of migration in Australia

To investigate any patterns of migration in Australia, we first see how the percentage of international permanent arrivals each state took over the time period 2022-23. Rather unsurprisingly, the two most populous states of Victoria and New South Wales received the most international arrivals, with 32% and 34% respectively. As we can see from the line graph below, most of these permanent arrivals are skilled workers, while another large category is for family reasons.

An interesting observation is the rapid rise in skilled permanent labour in the Northern Territory. This is no coincidence. In fact, in 2015, the Territory Government introduced a new policy called the "Designated Area Migration Agreement" (DAMA), in an attempt to address labour shortages in industries such as dentistry, nursing, and more recently, software engineers and teachers. Lasting from 2015 to 2019, this agreement saw over 280 employers be granted access to temporary skilled labourers, with an accelerated process to permanent residency. In late 2023, this agreement has been further renewed for 2024, and negotiations are underway for another renewal.

Another interesting observation has been each state's response to the drop in migration due to the COVID-19 pandemic. Whereas some states like Victoria have mostly returned to pre-pandemic levels of migration, other states such as Western Australia have not returned to a historical average level of overseas migration. This has led to skilled worker shortages and has seen the WA government move to address the issue. In a deal with the Federal government, roughly 10,000 skilled migration places have been allocated to WA in the financial year 2024-25.

Turning our attention now to temporary migration, we investigate the levels of international students entering Australia over a short period of time. The proportional symbol map on the left shows the number of international students that each university accepted. Universities have been grouped by their ABS statistical area level 4 (SA4). An example would be University of Melbourne, which would be in the region of "Melbourne - inner". Australia is internationally known for its universities and education. This is reflect in the concentration of international students around major capital city hubs. For instance, we see that in 2018, an inner Melbourne university accepted 101,522 international students, while an inner south Sydney university accepted 119,212 international students in 2018. This demonstrates the importance of international students not only on the livelihoods of businesses in major capital hubs, but also in smaller regional hubs, like Townsville and Cairns.
